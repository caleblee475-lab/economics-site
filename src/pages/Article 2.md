## Executive Summary

1. **AI as a Force Multiplier:** Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) operates as a profound macroeconomic force multiplier, fundamentally altering corporate cost architectures and promising significant long-term boosts to Multifactor Productivity (MFP), the efficiency of combined labor and capital inputs.  
2. **Short-term Structural Issues:** As companies and employees adapt to rapidly changing costs and re-valuation of skillsets, Singapore’s economy is currently navigating a complex feedback loop of structural skills mismatches and cyclical corporate restructuring.  
3.  **The Underemployment Issue:** As AI-induced margin compressions force corporate austerity and hiring freezes, we can expect depressed wages and underemployment for fresh graduates unsuited to the changing job market. Compressed starting salaries and a drop in permanent placements have driven a microeconomic reliance on alternative credit, alongside macroeconomic pressures on government stimulus.  
4. **Long-term Recovery:** Current economic contractions are a temporary effect as the market restructures to fit the ‘new normal’. However, Singapore’s pre-existing AI-ready infrastructure and proactive government investment has spurred significant Foreign Direct Investments (FDI) in advanced job creation and tertiary education, hastening the creation of high paying jobs in the AI sector. Hence, Singapore is expected to bypass the worst of the structural economic bust and leapfrog into the recovery arc of the Fourth Industrial Revolution.

## AI, Force Multipliers and Productivity Growth

The term ‘Force Multiplier’ refers to a factor that increases the productivity and efficiency of a production unit without the traditional scaled increase in production and labour costs. Artificial Intelligence (AI) exemplifies this in the workplace, increasing employee productivity through the automation of menial tasks, streamlining of workflows and accelerating execution of assigned taskings [1]. Hence, AI bolsters the capacity and productive efficiency of the workforce, leading to increased profits, decreased needs for manpower, or both.

## AI and (Un)employment: Beyond Structural Limits

Local adoption of AI tools by businesses and workers remains upbeat; AI integration within Small-to-Medium Enterprises (SMEs) has tripled from 4.2% to 14.5% over the last fiscal year, while non-SME adoption surged to 62.5%. Approximately 73.8% of workers report using AI tools to automate administrative and routine taskings [2].

However, the increased productivity rate per worker has resulted in lower demand for skilled labour, particularly for administrative and white-collar roles. Exacerbated by corporate cost-cutting priorities and margin compressions amidst the current economic downturn and energy cost-push inflation, the integration of AI has caused Singapore’s labour market to experience acute friction and contractions.

To fully appreciate the complexity of AI’s impact on Singapore’s labour market, we must examine the causes, effects and severity of the 3 most prevalent forms of unemployment observed so far.

### Structural Unemployment: The Skills Gap

The IMF has identified structural vulnerabilities in Singapore’s labour market as early as 2024, predicting that lower-complexity roles such as clerical and support jobs are in the high-exposure, low-complementarity subset. The report estimates that 38.6% of Singapore’s workforce was then at immediate risk of direct machine substitution and replacement. With AI being readily adopted at a pace faster than expected, we can expect this figure to increase in 2026. [3]

An exacerbating factor would be the observation that such High-Exposure, Low-Complementarity occupations are concentrated in PMET jobs, chiefly Professional, Technical and Executive roles. While managerial positions stand to benefit the most from the adoption of AI tools, most PMET roles are at a significant risk of AI replacement as early as 2024 to 2025 according to the IMF report.

With PMET jobs making up 64.2% of employed residents in Singapore, the significant shocks to the PMET job sector brought by AI-induced redundancy would naturally spread to most of Singapore’s labour market, expanding the employment downturn to a national scale.

### Business Cycles: Cyclical Cooling or True Unemployment?

Historical GDP growth data demonstrates that the economy is currently in a rebound phase, exhibiting patterns that strongly resemble the previous 2009 to 2020 expansionary cycle. However, as forward-looking indicators signal an impending macroeconomic cooling phase, corporations are preemptively adopting defensive fiscal postures. To prepare for the cooling period, hiring freezes and targeted job cuts have become commonplace as firms are incentivized to integrate AI expeditiously to permanently reduce variable staffing costs through automation.

This adjusted data confirms that cyclical trends are not the primary root cause of the rising unemployment figures, but as a catalyst to structural unemployment brought on by AI and skilled automation.

While the broader economy may currently enjoy positive growth in the short-term, the imminent cooling cycle will see corporations increasingly substitute human workers with automated agents and AI tools. If this is a permanent shift, we could observe a decoupling of Singapore’s economic output from employment rates, solidifying structural underemployment across the workforce as firms’ capital replaces human labour with AI tools and automated workflows.

Though Singapore’s labour market rarely suffers from significant unemployment rates, the combined effect of a cooling economy and structural disruptions in the labour market from employee-employer skills mismatch may present itself in a trend of growing underemployment instead of outright unemployment.

## Un(der)employment: A View From Both Microeconomic and Macroeconomic Lenses

The primary labor market threat for fresh graduates is not unemployment, but rather underemployment. As AI tools and automation pushes rapidly absorb routine entry-level cognitive tasks, exacerbated by cyclical downturns inducing hiring freezes, fresh graduates are increasingly pushed into suboptimal roles to secure employment.

Currently, fresh graduate employment rates have fallen noticeably from the post-pandemic recovery years of 2021 and 2022, dropping to 83.4% from a high of 94.4%. Full-time permanent employment has seen a similar trend of dropping to 74.4% from 87.5%, whereas part-time and temporary employment has steadily increased to 7.2%. [6] The rise in temporary and fall in full-time employment shows a decrease in both the quality and quantity of job placements for fresh graduates over the years.

### Microeconomic Implications: Sub-inflationary Wage Growth and Increasing Consumer Debt

In the short-term, the observed structural and cyclical underemployment figures show a trend of stagnating salaries amidst rising inflation as the CPI increases, albeit at a decreasing rate. With the median gross monthly salary remaining steady, graduates may experience sub-inflationary wage growth, leading to reduced individual purchasing power and a slower rise in household incomes, theoretically limiting consumer spending. However, youth consumer habits remain unchanged, with increased reliance on alternative debt vehicles such as Buy Now, Pay Later schemes as avenues of liquidity. [7] Further research is required to examine the true effect increased consumer debt has on the local economy.  
However, this wage depression is primarily a short-term issue as it stems from fresh graduates’ lack of specialised, non-automable skillsets. As graduates accumulate work experience via entry-level jobs or internships and are able to develop more in-depth skillsets, this issue is expected to dissipate as experienced graduates are able to command a wage premium in the labour market.

### Macroeconomic Impacts: Reduced Consumer Confidence and Increased Government Spending

In the January 2026 Ipsos Global Consumer Confidence Index, Singapore recorded a significant -3.7% decline in consumer confidence month-on-month (Dec 2025/Jan 2026), and a -5.1% year-on-year (2025/2026), one of the steepest trends in the Asia-Pacific region. [8]. This reflects how despite the availability of alternative debt vehicles and its increased usage, consumer spending has generally grown defensive. As consumer consumption is restrained by lower purchasing power, aggregate demand falls, fostering a more pessimistic economic environment.

To defend against a severe contraction in aggregate demand from suppressed wages and a lower domestic multiplier effect caused by reduced consumer spending, an expansionary fiscal policy was executed by the government. In Singapore’s Budget 2026, total government spending is projected to swell by 8.0% year-on-year to a record $154.69 billion [9].

A significant portion of this increase in government spending is allocated to domestic stimulus, namely in the form of the Enhanced Cost-of-Living Special Payment as a form of direct cash transfers to the general populace, and the expediting of Community Development Council (CDC) vouchers as targeted subsidies to encourage heartland and staple good spending, the two areas worst hit by cost-push inflation and reduced domestic spending [10].

### Fresh Graduate Underemployment: Transitional Growing Pains of the AI-Economy

Though significant, the negative effects surrounding fresh graduate underemployment are expected to be acute and soften over time as tertiary graduates and Institutes of Higher Learning (IHLs) adapt to the structural realities of an AI-integrated job market. 

With compulsory AI modules in all IHLs by 2027 to cultivate familiarity and proficiency in using AI to automate lower-level tasks [11], newer graduates are structurally insulated from the worst effects of the underemployment crisis caused by AI. By the time the affected graduates are able to acclimatise and gain sufficient experience to qualify for mid-level experienced roles which no longer suffer from the malus of depressed wages, their ‘freshly graduated’ counterparts would have already experienced a newer, AI-ready syllabus and have incorporated AI operational techniques into their workflow and skillsets, mostly remedying the underemployment issue as the market adjusts naturally. This ensures that the structural underemployment issue lasts no more than one to two cohorts as the syllabus adjusts to match students’ skillsets with market demands.

While firms’ AI-focused mindsets and priorities on using AI and automation to replace junior, cognitively simple tasks initially devalues inexperienced graduate labor and depresses wages in the short-term, the long-term optimisation of corporate workflows and production processes should cause MFP to spike. This positive effect is expected to be amplified as AI-ready graduates are progressively introduced into the labour market over time, where both labour and capital inputs enjoy greater productive efficiency due to the high complementarity of future roles and the ability of future graduates to maximise the positive impacts of AI tools.

## Lessons from the Dot-Com Bubble: Parallels from Computing in the Fourth Industrial Revolution

The presence of new technological advances displacing workers is not a new issue. During the 1990s, the widespread computerisation and basic automation of menial tasks via rudimentary algorithms displaced clerical workers, but created new sectors in software engineering, information systems and hardware development. Typically, this can be expected to result in higher-paying jobs being created in correspondence to the higher skills required of employees.

While the high-tech sector in Silicon Valley lost 16.5% of its jobs during the 2001-2008 bust, the average annual wage for remaining roles and newly created jobs in burgeoning technological industries surged by 36% [12]. The shedding of lower-level roles allowed the sector to pivot towards more advanced software and scientific research and production, clearing the local labour market for a higher skill and wage equilibrium.

## Solow’s Productivity Paradox, General Purpose Technologies (GPT) and Time Lag: 

Though technological advancements bring enormous productivity gains and economic growth in the long-term, such benefits usually follow years after their implementation by firms. Robert Solow’s Productivity Paradox was concerned with the early computerisation era of the 1980s, and why the economic benefits of computers were only felt years and decades after their expensive implementation in firms. The paradox defines a GPT as a technology that “does not directly increase productivity”, but does so once efforts are made to “develop new firm-specific technology with the GPT”. [13] 

Solow’s paradox theorises that MFP gains usually have a substantial time lag between the implementation of new GPTs and receiving their actual economic benefits as governments and enterprises require years of adaptation and integration of GPTs into their workflow and processes before any significant productivity gains can be observed. The paradox narrows this lag to three main factors:

1. **Reallocation of skilled labour:** High-skilled workers have to learn how to use the new GPT, leading to temporary productivity slow-downs as they reallocate their time “from production to knowledge acquisition”. As these skilled workers produce less output and require more resources spent learning and integrating the new technologies, there is a net drain on the total output, further hampering and slowing short-term economic growth.  
     
   When AI and automated algorithms first debuted, highly skilled workers, executives and engineers were reallocated from immediate revenue-generating roles towards learning about the potential of AI in streamlining and automating menial processes in the ‘knowledge accumulation’ phase.  
2. **Development and Implementation Delays:** In accommodating new GPTs that require specialised firm-specific infrastructure to fully capitalise on potential productivity gains, the building of such custom applications takes considerable time and further resources.  
     
   In the case of modern AI and Large Language Models (LLMs), firms did not gain any appreciable productivity bonuses from the usage of publicly available AI LLMs until the technology’s pivot towards enterprises allowed for business-focused functionalities and industry-specific models were refined and released.   
3. **Initial Inefficiencies:** Immediately after integration into the workflow and production processes, the steep learning curve of the new technology causes firms to initially be less productive as workers require time to acclimatise to the new process and optimise their individual workflow.  
     
   Early adopters of AI discovered that the new technology was prone to hallucinations and required substantial human oversight and guidance. Firms also had to update legacy administrative systems and standardise data formats for AI integration, further lowering initial MFP before the systems were refined and errors fixed.

## An AI-Ready Singapore: Accelerating the Adoption Process

However, the long and expensive integration process identified by Solow’s Paradox may be mitigated by Singapore’s proactive approach towards implementing AI and automated workflows in the public sector, businesses and education. The government’s aggressive push towards AI adoption on a national scale can be identified in 4 main directions:

1. **AI-Ready Infrastructure:** Singapore currently leads Southeast Asia in AI data centre construction and operation [14], enjoying a knowledge and skills monopoly by concentrating experts and their intellectual capital within its borders.  
2. **Preset Goals and Identified Specialisations:** The government is prioritising the use of AI in four key sectors, namely connectivity, advanced manufacturing, healthcare and finance. As these areas currently constitute “over 40% of our (Singapore’s) GDP” [15], focused and targeted integration guarantees that productivity gains yield the highest macroeconomic returns in the long-run. By establishing a detailed roadmap with identified specialisations the time-to-value period can be shortened as purpose-built, industry-specific AI technologies are significantly faster for workers to learn and adapt to than generic iterations of publicly available models and tools.  
3. **Incentivising Private Capital and Investments in Education:** In addition to government spending and state investment, substantial foreign capital is also funding Singapore’s transition towards AI. FDI inflows into Singapore reached a record $197 billion in 2025, recording a year-on-year increase of 8.4% [16]. In 2026, Multinational Technology Corporations such as Amazon and OpenAI announced a series of substantial planned investments into Singapore’s infrastructure and economy, primarily in the form of job creation for AI-related sectors with innovation and talent pipelines, entrenching local intellectual capital. [17,18] To guarantee a long-term supply of graduates with AI-ready skillsets, Microsoft has announced a $5.5 billion educational package to support tertiary students and educators in the adoption and usage of AI tools from 2025 to 2029 [19].  
     
   By capitalising on Singapore’s stable economy and strong reputation through early investments and construction of AI-ready infrastructure, the government has built the foundation for private capital to stimulate the economy via the organic creation of AI-era jobs. Responsive and targeted supply-side initiatives via tertiary curriculum overhauls to encompass AI-ready skills directly addresses the skills mismatch and issue of structural underemployment in the long-run, ensuring minimal disruption to the labour market. Thus, the market-driven demand and government interventions in ensuring a steady long-term supply of AI-augmented skills could help mitigate the worst effects of Solow’s “economic restructuring phase” as the nation transitions to the AI-based era. Rather than enduring the prolonged productivity lag and structural underemployment from integrating AI as a GPT, Singapore’s economy is positioned to bypass the downward adoption cycle and accelerate directly into an AI-augmented, high-productivity recovery arc.

## Conclusion

We can maintain a cautiously optimistic outlook regarding long-term positive growth for both Singapore’s broader economy and its labor market. As the workforce graduates from the initial frictions of AI deployment and adapts to a more skills-demanding ecosystem, the resulting multifactor productivity boom will naturally clear the path for a highly resilient, higher-paying labor market. This structural upgrade will subsequently drive a permanent, long-term increase in aggregate domestic spending, proving that the integration of AI into the modern economy is ultimately a long-term net positive that outweighs the short-term transitionary downsides.
