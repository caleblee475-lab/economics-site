## Executive Summary

1. **Tariff Cost Burden Shifts:** The sudden rise in energy prices as past contracts locking in pre-Hormuz closure prices expire is passed down onto consumers as companies struggle to maintain profit margins. Discretionary consumer spending is expected to fall as the cost-of-living increases.
2. **Cost-push Inflation:** The continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz and its disruption of global supply chains has caused cost-push inflation on electricity and consumer goods, driving prices upward.
3. **Hormuz & Labour Market Effects:** Compressed profit margins lower the marginal revenue product of labor, leading to stagnant wages and hiring freezes as businesses adapt to a harsher economy.
4. **Future-proofing Singapore:** While alternative energy sources and diversification efforts plan to mitigate negative effects both in the short and long-term, derived demand from AI data centres may prolong Singapore’s structural vulnerability to external energy shocks.

## Tariff Revisions: The Immediate Impacts

1. **Quarterly Revision Intervals:** The sudden jump in energy prices, projected by industry experts to fall within a 20-25% volatility band, is due to the fact that the energy tariff is revised quarterly [1]. This means that existing energy contracts had locked-in prices set pre-Hormuz, with electricity companies absorbing the inflated costs. However, as these contracts start to end, future tariffs reflect the immediate economic situation, causing prices to jump sharply.
2. **Energy Import Dependency:** Singapore’s electricity prices are "tied to imported gas costs" [1]. As international demand heightens over fewer natural gas suppliers, local energy prices can be expected to rise by a significant margin as international competition exacerbates the shortage.
3. **Electricity as an Inelastic Staple Good:** As an essential, price-inelastic good, the increased cost burden is transferred to households. SP Group has revised a 2.1% increment in household electricity tariffs for the April-June 2026 quarter [2], showing that any imported cost-push inflation has an immediate effect on market prices.

## Macroeconomic Spillovers Beyond the Electricity Market: The CPI and Labour Market

1. **Universal Input Inflation:** Involved in the manufacturing process of every product, the cost-push electricity inflation can be observed to create aftershocks in the consumer goods market. As electricity prices climb, firms have to contend with rising variable costs. For staple and essential goods, this will result in the additional costs being passed downstream, similar to the energy market, and a corresponding rise in the Consumer Price Index is expected.

Currently, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) reflects an overall month-on-month increase of 0.7% from April to May 2026 [3].
* Despite a 2.4% MOM rise in housing and utilities spending, utilities and other fuels experienced a contraction of 0.1% MOM. This can be attributed to government intervention through U-Save and S&CC rebates. Such schemes help to keep the energy market’s cost-push inflation in check, temporarily suppressing household expenditure rises [4].

* Indirect inflationary effects can be seen in secondary markets such as transportation. Without government subsidies, transport spending is up by 7.4% YOY, led by private transport expenditure rising by 8.6% YOY.

2. **Margin Compression in Exporting Sectors:** For export-focused price-taker firms on the international market, price changes are ill-advised to maintain export competitiveness. Instead, such firms must absorb the inflated costs, resulting in tighter profit margins and temporary deficits.

3. **Labour Market Contraction:** As firms grapple with normal and subnormal profit levels, the marginal revenue product of labor of employees is expected to fall similarly. As worker productivity remains stagnant amid rising manufacturing costs, businesses are incentivised to minimise employee expenditures. We can expect this to manifest in several effects, such as:
    * **Hiring freezes:** With nearly 60% of employers planning hiring freezes as early as end-2025 [5], we can expect this trend to be even more prevalent.
    * **Sub-inflationary wage growth:** With more firms keeping wages stagnant and fewer firms implementing smaller wage growths [6] coupled with expectations of rising inflation [7], the possibility of inflation outpacing wage growth in general remains elevated, threatening real consumer purchasing power.
    * **Corporate merging, restructuring and downsizing:** Retrenchments have climbed to a "Three-Year High" amidst restructuring and downsizing [8], with fewer job vacancies to replace retrenched workers and accommodate new graduates [9].

## Supply-side Mitigation Policies: Alternative Energy Sources

1. **Nuclear Energy Feasibility Testing:** Singapore is looking to lessen reliance on external markets for energy in the long-term with the initiation of Phase 1 of the Integrated Nuclear Infrastructure review, examining the feasibility of small modular reactors (SMRs) [11,12]. If successfully implemented, we can expect a long-term reduction in the price ceiling of electricity once the SMRs are online.
2. **Expansion of Renewable Energy Sectors:** To reduce reliance on fossil fuels, Singapore’s green energy sector has seen a considerable expansion, with a near fivefold rise in solar capacity "in the last five years" [12,13]. The more Singapore’s energy needs can be offloaded onto green and nuclear energy sources, the less vulnerable the energy market is to external shocks and supply disruptions.

## Potential Weakpoints: Data Centres and Future Energy Prices

With the AI boom and Singapore’s push for AI integration both in the workspace and economy to become the region’s digital-AI hub [14], we can expect a positive impact on the economy as companies vie to open AI data centres, increasing foreign direct investment and creating high-skilled job vacancies. However, the considerable energy consumption of such facilities carries immediate energy sustainability concerns.

1. **Immediate energy consumption levels:** Singapore currently accounts for 54% of Southeast Asia’s energy consumption in AI data centres [15,16]. Across the border, Johor’s data centres are expected to consume up to 40% of the state’s total electricity demand by 2035 [17].

2. **Compounding short-term vulnerabilities:** As data centres are constructed and brought online in the short-term, the vastly increased demand for energy outpaces the slower expansion and diversification of Singapore’s energy sourcing. This is likely to force a greater reliance on natural gas, exacerbating Singapore's current dependencies on the international energy market and making external supply shocks more acute.
